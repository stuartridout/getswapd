02 · Typography

One font does almost everything

Figtree carries 99% of the product. Clean, warm, geometric sans with slightly rounded terminals. Weighted 400 to 900, it handles every surface from 10px nav labels to 72px hero display. Fraunces Italic arrives at specific voice moments — the challenge question, empty-state nudges, occasional subtitle flourishes. It is the product's voice rendered typographically. Everywhere else, restraint.

03 · Voice in the UI

Two registers, typographically separated

Every piece of copy in Swapd falls into one of two jobs. Functional UI tells you what to tap and what happened. Voice is the product talking to you as a friend — cheeky empty states, warm challenge questions, the little nudges that make it feel human. The font change is the signal: plain Figtree for function, Fraunces Italic for voice. This is the single most important typographic rule in the product. Follow it and users learn to tell the two apart without thinking.

Functional UI · Figtree
Buttons, labels, counts, timestamps, statuses. Plain English, short, accurate. The product as an app.

05 · Shadows & depth

Four shadow tokens, applied everywhere

Soft drop shadows with realistic blur — cards float above the surface, never stamp onto it. Four levels cover every case: sm for quiet items (list rows, avatars), md for standard surfaces (lavender callouts, notif cards), card for photo tiles with weight (polaroid stacks, memory frames), lift for primary action (CTAs, FABs, active pills). No hard offset shadows. No borders doing shadow work.

shadow-mdcallouts · cards · surfaces

06 · Buttons & pills

Pill-shaped. Three tones. Nothing else.

Every interactive element is a pill. The colour tells you its weight. Teal for primary action. White for secondary. Pink only when there's a real attention moment — used sparingly so it means something when it appears. Ghost buttons (no background, just text) for low-stakes navigation. No square corners. No outlined buttons. No gradients.

Button variants
Sized for comfortable thumb-tap on mobile. 14px/22px padding on standard, 8px/14px on small. Shadow-lift on primary and attention, shadow-md on secondary.
Pills & tags
Smaller than buttons — 7px/14px padding, 12px type, 700 weight. Filter states use teal-solid for active, white for inactive. Lavender for lightweight status. Pink-solid for counts only.

07 · Polaroid states

Four states, one shape

Every Swap in the list is represented by a polaroid stack — two offset, tilted photo cards. Hers in front, yours behind. What fills the photo rectangle tells you the state. The stack itself never changes shape; only what it contains. That consistency is why users can scan the list at a glance.

08 · Motion

Restrained, purposeful, never decorative

Motion exists only where it does emotional or informational work. A Reveal is worth a small animated moment because it's the emotional core of the product. Scrolling is not. Buttons shouldn't bounce; they should respond. If an animation doesn't change how the user feels or what they understand, it shouldn't be there.

Do animate
  • The Reveal — both polaroids drop in slightly staggered, settle with tiny rotation. The only "wow" moment. 400ms total.
  • Your turn card — on appear, subtle pulse of the pink kicker text. Once, then stop. Draws the eye without being needy.
  • FAB — scale + shadow on press. Standard iOS spring.
  • Polaroid state changes — crossfade when a Swap moves from "waiting" to "ready". 200ms.
  • Filter pill active — 150ms ease-out on colour and shadow change.
Don't animate
  • Scrolling content — no parallax, no fade-in-on-scroll, no sticky hero effects. The path in Memory Lane is visually rich enough without animated sparkle.
  • Empty states — the voice nudge should feel like it's been sitting there waiting for you, not hop in.
  • Typography — no typed-out effect, no word-by-word reveals. Read the words when they appear.
  • Icons — static. No spinning hearts, no bouncing bells. Earn every animation.
  • Loading states — subtle teal dot pulse, nothing more. Skeleton screens are fine but never animated shimmer.
09 · Principles

Seven rules the UI obeys

These are the rules every design decision is tested against. When in doubt, go back to these. When adding something new, check it passes all seven before shipping.

Rule 01
Float, don't stamp.
Soft drop shadows with blur. Cards rest lightly on paper, not stuck down. No hard offset shadows. No thick outlines. Depth from blur, never from borders.
Rule 02
Ration the pink.
Pink is the loudest colour in the system. Use only for attention — unread dots, "your turn" kickers, the Reveal question mark. If pink is everywhere, nothing is pink.
Rule 03
Variety within discipline.
Polaroids tilt, shapes vary, memory frames rotate. But palette, radius, and shadow tokens are identical across every surface. The rules are what let the variety feel playful, not random.
Rule 04
Candy, never cartoon.
Pool teal, hot pink, lavender, white — confident and joyful, but no mascots, no comic-sans, no kids'-TV shapes. A thirty-five-year-old feels at home. So does a fifteen-year-old. Neither feels patronised.
Rule 05
The photos are the stars.
Every UI element sits around the photos, not on top. White frame, slight tilt, soft shadow — they read as physical prints. The Reveal lets the two photos do the work; the chrome disappears.
Rule 06
One font does the work.
Figtree at weights 400–900 handles every surface. Fraunces Italic appears only for voice moments — challenge questions, empty-state nudges, named captions. Two typefaces, absolute discipline. The restraint is the character.
Rule 07
Functional in Figtree, voice in Fraunces.
The font change is the signal. Plain Figtree for buttons, labels, statuses. Fraunces Italic for anything that's the product talking to you as a friend. Users learn the difference without being told.

Accessibility baseline
  • Contrast
    All text on white passes WCAG AA at its size. Teal on white = 7.1:1. Pink on white = 3.1:1 (large text only — do not use pink for body copy).
  • Tap targets
    Minimum 44×44pt for all interactive elements. Pills/buttons comfortably exceed this.
  • Motion preference
    Respect prefers-reduced-motion. The Reveal becomes a static reveal without the drop-in animation.
  • Dynamic Type
    All type scales proportionally. No fixed heights on text containers.
  • Colour-blind
    State changes (locked, waiting, ready, revealed) never rely on colour alone — each has a distinct icon or photo state.
